Addition

Addition is the process of adding two or more numbers together to get a total.
For example, 2 + 2 = 4

A number line is a great visual tool for teaching addition with an easy method. It’s especially useful for children first learning how to add small numbers together. A number line is like using stabilisers before riding a bike. The visual aid will give children the confidence to eventually add together numbers in their head.

To use a number line, start at the lowest number in the problem and count up the line until you reach the second number. The number you reach will be your answer.

This handy trick is a more advanced kind of number line that can be used for adding two large numbers together.

To use the jump strategy, start from the biggest number and jump forward in groups of ten until you’ve used all the tens up. Then add the ones until you’ve reached the answer. This strategy makes adding large numbers easy by separating tens and ones into manageable chunks.

Bridging through ten is a mental maths technique for adding two numbers with a total greater than ten. Pupils count through to ten then add the remaining numbers.

For example, let’s take the sum 8 + 6. Your class can use the bridging through ten method like this:

8 + 2 = 10 which leaves 4 remaining

10 + 4 is the equivalent of 8 + 2

So, 8 + 6 = 14

This type of calculation is perfect for younger students who are still getting familiar with double-digit numbers. Once memorised, using this method to break down double-digit additions will strengthen their knowledge of number bonds until they can recall them at a moment’s notice

Subtraction

Subtraction is the process of taking one number away from another to get a total.
For example, 4 - 2 = 2

Multiplication

Multiplication is the process of adding a number to itself a certain number of times.
For example, 2 x 2 = 4

Division

Division is the process of splitting a number into equal parts.
For example, 4 ÷ 2 = 2
